Title Washing Scams: How Crooks Clean Bad Titles and How to Spot Them

By Manny T. · May 28, 2026 · 10 min read

Title washing is one of the dirtiest tricks in the used car business. A car that has been totaled in a flood or declared a total loss can be "washed" through another state and sold with a clean title to an unsuspecting buyer.

Here's exactly how it works and how you can protect yourself.

How Title Washing Works

1. Car is wrecked or flooded in State A (e.g. Florida after a hurricane). Insurance totals it.

2. The salvage title is issued.

3. The car is sold at auction to a flipper.

4. The flipper registers it in State B (e.g. Georgia or Tennessee) that has lax rules or doesn't check previous brands properly.

5. A "clean" title is issued in the new state.

6. The car is sold in State C as "clean title, one owner" on Craigslist or Facebook Marketplace.

Red Flags That Suggest Title Washing

  • The title is from a different state than where the car is being sold, with no clear explanation.
  • The seller has "just moved" or "bought it for a friend" stories.
  • Price is suspiciously low for the year/miles/condition.
  • Seller pushes hard for a quick cash deal in a parking lot instead of at their home or a bank.
  • The car has fresh paint or carpet in an older vehicle.
  • Odometer seems too low for the wear on the car.

How to Protect Yourself

Always run a full title history report before you go see the car. Look at the entire chain of title, not just the current title. If it has bounced between states after a major storm or auction, walk away.

Get the VIN upfront and check NMVTIS and other databases. A good report will flag branded titles even if they were washed in certain states.

Never buy a car without seeing the physical title in the seller's name.

Before you go see any car, run a VIN report first.
