How to Negotiate a Used Car Like a Pro (Word-for-Word Scripts)

By Manny T. · June 1, 2026 · 11 min read

Most buyers are terrible at negotiating. They get nervous, they accept the first counter, or they get emotionally attached to the car and overpay. The dealer or private seller counts on that.

Negotiation is a skill, and like any skill, it can be learned with the right scripts and mindset. Here are the exact tactics and word-for-word lines I used when buying cars for the dealership — flipped to work for you as the buyer.

Prep Before You Even Talk Price

Never negotiate without ammo. Before you discuss price:

  • Have your TrustCar report (or at least the local value range).
  • Know the car's actual market value in your ZIP.
  • Have a maximum number in your head — and be willing to walk if they won't meet it.
  • Point out issues calmly from your inspection or report.

The Opening Offer Script

Don't say "What's your best price?" That's weak. Instead:

"I've done my research and based on the issues I'm seeing plus local comps, I'm prepared to offer $X. What do you think?"

Then shut up. Silence is your friend. Let them respond first.

When They Counter

Typical response: "I can't go that low."

Your reply: "I understand. What number would make you comfortable?"

Or: "Help me understand what I'm missing. The report shows the value is around $Y and there are these specific issues. What would it take to get this done today?"

The Walk-Away Script

If they're not moving: "I appreciate your time, but this isn't going to work for me at this price. If you change your mind, here's my number. Thanks."

Then actually leave or hang up. 70% of the time they will call back within 24 hours with a better number.

Pro Tips from the Inside

  • Always negotiate the out-the-door price, not just the sale price. "What's your best out-the-door number including all fees and taxes?"
  • Never negotiate when you're in love with the car. Bring a friend who can be the bad guy.
  • Use the report as your neutral third party: "According to the data..." sounds more objective than "I think..."

Negotiation isn't about being a jerk. It's about being informed, calm, and willing to walk. The person who is willing to walk away almost always gets the better deal.
